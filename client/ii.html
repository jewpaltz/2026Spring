The Order of the Seder

A Guide Through the Fifteen Steps of Freedom
1 Kadesh | Sanctification

The journey begins by elevating our physical surroundings into the realm of the holy. We fill the first of four cups of wine and recite the Kiddush, sanctifying the festival and expressing gratitude for the gift of this time. This act reminds us that even in the mundane, there is potential for holiness.

2 Urchatz | Washing

We wash our hands in a ritual manner without reciting the customary blessing. This unusual act is designed to pique the curiosity of the children and signals that the evening will be different from all other nights, governed by laws of purity and mindfulness.

3 Karpas | The Appetizer

A small piece of vegetable, often parsley or potato, is dipped into salt water and eaten. The green symbolizes the flourishing of spring and rebirth, while the salt water represents the bitter tears shed by our ancestors during their long years of toil in Egypt.

4 Yachatz | Breaking

The leader breaks the middle of the three matzahs on the Seder plate. The smaller part is returned to the plate as the "bread of affliction," while the larger portion is wrapped and set aside as the Afikoman, representing the hidden redemption we hope to find by the end of the night.

5 Maggid | The Telling

This is the core of the Seder, where we fulfill the commandment to tell the story of the Exodus. We ask the Four Questions, recount the Ten Plagues, and discuss the miracles of our liberation, ensuring the legacy of freedom is passed from one generation to the next.

6 Rachtzah | Washing

We wash our hands once more, this time with the traditional blessing, "Al Netilat Yadayim." This prepares us for the physical and spiritual consumption of the Matzah, the primary mitzvah of the evening's meal.

7 Motzi | The Blessing

Before eating the Matzah, we recite the "HaMotzi" blessing, thanking the Almighty for bringing forth bread from the earth. This acknowledges the physical sustenance provided to us, even in the simplest of forms.

8 Matzah | The Commandment

A second, specific blessing is recited over the Matzah, focusing on the commandment to eat unleavened bread. We eat a portion of the top and middle matzahs while leaning to the left, a posture that symbolizes the luxury and comfort of free people.

9 Maror | Bitter Herbs

We dip bitter herbs, usually horseradish or romaine lettuce, into the sweet Charoset. Though we are now free, we must never forget the sharp, stinging bitterness of slavery that our ancestors endured, keeping our empathy for the oppressed alive.

10 Korech | The Hillel Sandwich

Following the ancient custom of the sage Hillel, we combine Matzah and Maror into a sandwich. By eating the bread of freedom and the herbs of slavery together, we acknowledge that joy and sorrow are often intertwined in the human experience.

11 Shulchan Orech | The Set Table

The festive meal is served, and we celebrate our freedom with a joyous banquet. It is a time for family and friends to enjoy traditional foods and the warmth of community, reflecting the abundance that comes with liberty.

12 Tzafun | The Hidden

The Afikoman, hidden earlier in the evening, is now brought back to the table and eaten as the final taste of the Seder. It represents the "Passover Offering" of old and signifies that the ultimate redemption may be hidden, but it is within our reach.

13 Barech | Grace After Meals

We conclude the meal by reciting Birkat Hamazon, the Grace After Meals, over the third cup of wine. We thank the Creator for the food we have eaten and the land we inhabit, recognizing that all our needs are provided for.

14 Hallel | Songs of Praise

We fill the fourth cup of wine and open the door for Elijah the Prophet. Together, we sing Psalms of praise and gratitude, celebrating the miracles of the past and expressing our hope for a future of universal peace and redemption.

15 Nirtzah | Acceptance

The Seder concludes with the hope that our service has been accepted. We end the evening by looking toward the future, declaring in unison the ancient prayer of yearning: "L'shanah Haba'ah B'Yerushalayim"—Next Year in Jerusalem!

L'shanah Haba'ah B'Yerushalayim!
